## Introduction

Théxar's atmosphere is one of the key features making the planet habitable. Its composition, density, and dynamics are the result of a complex balance between geological processes, biological activity, and astrophysical factors.

Elevated CO₂ content, the presence of biogenic hydrogen sulfide (H₂S), the characteristic golden-bronze color of the sky, and a thinner ozone layer — each of these factors has played its role in the evolution of local life and the formation of civilization.

Théxar's atmosphere represents a "pristine" example of an equilibrium greenhouse state.

## 15.1 Atmospheric Composition

> **KEY FACT**
>
> The main distinction of Théxar's atmosphere is its CO₂ content of 0.8%. This is not the result of a catastrophe, but an **equilibrium greenhouse effect**: CO₂ compensates for the low insolation of the star Khar'Vex.

### 15.1.1 Chemical Composition of Dry Air

> **TABLE 15.1: Chemical composition of dry air at the surface**
>
> | Gas | Formula | Share (Théxar) | Role |
> |-----|---------|----------------|------|
> | Nitrogen | N₂ | 71.8% | Buffer gas |
> | Oxygen | O₂ | 22.6% | Respiration, photosynthesis |
> | Argon | Ar | 2.9% | Decay product of ⁴⁰K |
> | Carbon dioxide | CO₂ | **0.8%** | Greenhouse gas |
> | Water vapor | H₂O | 0.5–2.0% | Greenhouse gas |
> | Neon | Ne | 0.05% | Inert |
> | Methane | CH₄ | 0.0003% | Biogenic |
> | Hydrogen sulfide | H₂S | **0.0008%** | Biogenic, sky color |
> | Carbon monoxide | CO | 0.00005% | Volcanic |

The elevated argon content (2.9%) is a result of more intensive radiogenic decay of ⁴⁰K in Théxar's mantle. This indirectly confirms the planet's age and geological activity.

Hydrogen sulfide (H₂S) at a concentration of 0.0008% is a unique feature of Théxar. Its sources: volcanic emissions and the metabolism of chemosynthesizing bacteria. H₂S not only determines the color of the sky but also participates as an electron donor in alternative metabolic pathways, supporting unique ecosystems.

### 15.1.3 Ozone Layer

Théxar's ozone layer contains ~30% less O₃ per unit volume. Main reasons:

1. Lower O₂ content in the stratosphere (thin stratosphere)
2. Presence of H₂S, which reacts with ozone: H₂S + O₃ → SO₂ + H₂O
3. Elevated UV flux (due to proximity to the star)

**Consequence:** elevated UV-B flux at the surface has exerted significant evolutionary pressure on local life — Théxar's organisms possess high radiation resistance.

## 15.2 Sky Color

The color of Théxar's sky is one of the most striking visual features. It is determined by three factors:

### 15.2.1 Physics of Scattering

1. **CO₂** absorbs light in the blue region of the spectrum (absorption bands 350–450 nm), removing blue tones from scattered light
2. **H₂S** alters the scattering of shortwave radiation through the formation of sulfuric acid aerosols in the stratosphere
3. **Spectrum of Khar'Vex** — a K2V class star with an emission peak at ~570 nm (orange-yellow)

> **COMPARISON: Sky color**
>
> | Condition | Théxar | Physics |
> |-----------|--------|---------|
> | Noon | Golden-bronze | CO₂ absorption + Khar'Vex spectrum |
> | Sunset | Deep red | Long path through H₂S layer |
> | Twilight | Purple | H₂S luminescence in mesosphere |
> | Horizon | Bright orange | Suspended dust and aerosols |

### 15.2.2 Color Perception

The Xha'ri are tetrachromats with sensitivity in the 320–780 nm range. → see [Ch.05, "Anatomy"] Thanks to this, they distinguish up to 7 color bands in the sky (from ultraviolet halo to infrared glimmer), giving them an advantage in navigation at twilight.

### 15.3.2 Long-Term Stability

On Théxar, the key role in CO₂ regulation is played by:

1. **Volcanic degassing** — CO₂ input from the mantle (active tectonics)
2. **Biological fixation** — photosynthesis and chemosynthesis binding carbon
3. **Oceanic pump** — dissolution of CO₂ in the cold waters of Gharra

The balance of these processes maintains CO₂ concentration at 0.8% with fluctuations of ±0.05% over the last 500 million cycles.

## 15.4 Atmospheric Circulation

### 15.4.1 Global Circulation

Théxar has a 6-cell model of atmospheric circulation, but with important differences:

> **TABLE 15.4: Circulation cells**
>
> | Cell | Latitudes | Direction | Weather character |
> |------|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
> | Kharak (equatorial) | 0°–28° | Ascending | Abundant precipitation, jungles |
> | Tor (temperate) | 28°–55° | Descending | Dry zones, deserts |
> | Thel (polar) | 55°–90° | Weak | Cold dry air |

Due to slower rotation (27.3 h), the cells are wider and weaker:

- **Cell width:** 10–15% greater
- **Wind speeds:** 20–30% lower (average global speed ~2.4 mo/h)
- **Trade winds:** less pronounced but more stable
- **Monsoons:** practically absent due to weakened seasonal circulation

### 15.4.2 Jet Streams

Théxar's planetary jet streams:

| Stream | Latitude | Average speed | Height | Seasonality |
|--------|----------|---------------|--------|-------------|
| Northern polar | 55°N | 24.4 mo/h | 1.6–2.0 mo | Stronger in winter |
| Northern subtropical | 28°N | 16.3 mo/h | 2.0–2.4 mo | Stable |
| Southern subtropical | 28°S | 15.2 mo/h | 2.0–2.4 mo | Stable |
| Southern polar | 55°S | 28.5 mo/h | 1.6–2.0 mo | Stronger in winter |

### 15.4.3 Seasonal Variations

Théxar's axial tilt is 14.2°. This leads to:

- **Less pronounced seasons** — summer-winter difference in temperate latitudes is ~8.2°X
- **Weak seasonal wind shifts** — monsoon circulation is practically absent
- **Small day-length changes** — from 22.5 h (winter) to 32.1 h (summer) at latitude 45°

## 15.5 Weather Phenomena

### 15.5.1 Rain and Precipitation

Average annual precipitation on Théxar is ~880 mm/year. Distribution:

| Precipitation type | Share | Feature |
|--------------------|-------|---------|
| Rain | 74% | Slightly acidic (pH 5.2–5.8) due to CO₂ |
| Snow | 18% | At latitudes >45° and highlands |
| Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) | 6% | Near volcanic zones |
| Coal dust (dry) | 2% | In Kharak's deserts |

The slightly acidic nature of rain (pH 5.2–5.8) is due to the dissolution of CO₂ forming carbonic acid. This phenomenon is a planetary norm, not a result of pollution.

### 15.5.2 Tropical Cyclones

Frequency: 20–35 cyclones per year, but **intensity is lower** due to longer days weakening the Coriolis effect.

| Category | Wind speed | Frequency | Damage |
|----------|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Kh-1 (weak) | 12.8–24.0 mo/h | 12–15/year | Minimal |
| Kh-2 (moderate) | 24.2–36.0 mo/h | 6–8/year | Moderate |
| Kh-3 (strong) | 36.2–42.3 mo/h | 2–4/year | Significant |
| Kh-4 (very strong) | 42.5–51.0 mo/h | 0–1/year | Severe |
| Kh-5 (devastating) | >51.2 mo/h | 1 in 10–20 years | Catastrophic |

Cyclone season: month Gharra-Ven — month Thel-Mor.

### 15.5.3 Dust Storms

Dust storms are the most serious local weather threat on Théxar. They are significantly stronger due to:

- Lower air humidity in desert regions
- Active volcanic activity supplying fine ash
- Wider circulation cells creating sustained winds

The most powerful storms can:
- Transport up to 800 million tons of dust
- Color the sky a deep orange-red for 2–3 weeks
- Block up to 60% of starlight over an area of 0.2–0.3 million mo²
- Carry dust across the ocean from Kharak to Rhuval (distance >609.8 mo)

> **KEY FACT**
>
> Théxar's dust storms are the main factor in erosion and mineral transport. Approximately 1.5 billion ru of dust is transported between continents annually.

### 15.5.4 Electrical Phenomena

| Phenomenon | Frequency | Feature |
|------------|-----------|---------|
| Lightning | ~1.2 billion/year | High discharge energy |
| Corona discharge | Common | Glow on high peaks (corona effect) |
| Aurorae | Moderate | Observed down to latitude 35° |
| Volcanic lightning | Frequent | Accompanies ash emissions |

Aurorae on Théxar are observed at lower latitudes (down to 35°) due to the characteristics of the magnetosphere.

## 15.7 Long-Term Climate Cycles

Théxar's climate experiences periodic fluctuations caused by astronomical and geological factors.

### 15.7.1 Orbital Cycles

Théxar's orbital cycles include:

| Cycle | Period (years) | T amplitude | Cause |
|-------|---------------|-------------|-------|
| Eccentricity | ~95,000 | ±0.7°X | Orbital change |
| Axial tilt | ~41,000 | ±0.4°X | Axial nutation |
| Precession | ~38,600 | ±0.3°X | Equinox drift |

> **KEY FACT**
>
> Théxar's axial precession has a period of ~38,600 years, due to the planet's smaller mass with a comparable moment of inertia, making the axis more sensitive to tidal influences from the satellites Serrin and Thel.

### 15.7.2 Volcanic Winters

Major eruptions (VEI-7+) can temporarily alter the planet's climate:

| Event | Time | VEI | T drop | Duration |
|-------|------|-----|--------|----------|
| Tor-7 eruption | ~12,000 ya | 7.2 | −1.7°X | 4 yr |
| Raal supervolcano | ~48,000 ya | 8.1 | −3.2°X | 8 yr |
| Irkhen chain | ~3,890 Y.E. | 6.8 | −0.8°X | 2 yr |

Over the last 100,000 years, 5–7 events have been recorded, each leading to a global temperature decrease of 1.1–2.2°X for 2–5 years.

### 15.7.3 Current State

The modern climate of Théxar is in an **interglacial phase** that began ~12,000 years ago. The current average temperature (51.6°X) is close to the interglacial maximum. Polar caps occupy their minimum area in the last 100,000 years — 0.3 million mo².

The warming of the last 500 years is linked not to anthropogenic activity (the Xha'Ruun civilization's industry runs on fusion and renewable sources), but to a natural recovery from a mini-ice age phase.
